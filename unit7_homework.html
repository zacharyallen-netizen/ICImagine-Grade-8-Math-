Question 1
The model is y = 8x + 30 (x = training weeks, y = max push-ups in 1 min). Find the green dot: how many push-ups after 5 weeks?
y = 8(5) + 30 = 40 + 30 = 70 push-ups ✓
Question 2
In y = 8x + 30 (training weeks vs. push-ups), interpret the slope of 8.
Slope = rate of change: 8 more push-ups per week of training. ✓
Question 3
Interpret the y-intercept of 30 in the same model (y = 8x + 30).
y-intercept = y when x = 0: at zero weeks training, the model predicts 30 push-ups (baseline ability). ✓
Question 4
Temperature vs. electricity bills: y = −2x + 120 (x = °F above 50, y = bill in $). What does slope −2 mean?
Wait — actually a HIGHER temperature usually means more AC and HIGHER bills. A negative slope here would mean: for each extra degree above 50, bill goes DOWN by $2. In a real context, check your data carefully! In this equation as given: slope = −2 means bill decreases $2 per degree rise. ✓
Question 5
Using y = −2x + 120 from Q4: what is the predicted bill at 15 degrees above 50°F?
y = −2(15) + 120 = −30 + 120 = $90 ✓
Question 6
Model: y = 4x + 8 (x = hours tutoring, y = points gained). The data was collected for x = 1 to x = 6. Is predicting y at x = 10 an example of interpolation or extrapolation?
x = 10 is OUTSIDE the data range of 1–6 → extrapolation. Less reliable because the trend may not continue. ✓
Question 7
Using y = 4x + 8 from Q6, which is the most reliable prediction?
x = 4 is within the data range (1–6) → interpolation → most reliable prediction. ✓
Question 8
A researcher finds y = 50x + 200 models home price (y, in thousands $) based on square footage (x, in hundreds sq ft). What is the predicted price for a 1,500 sq ft home (x = 15)?
y = 50(15) + 200 = 750 + 200 = 950 (thousands) = $950,000 ✓

What is the joint frequency of 1st lunch students who prefer Soccer?
Joint frequency = interior cell: 1st Lunch × Soccer = 18 ✓

What is the marginal frequency for Basketball?

Question 3
What percent of 1st lunch students prefer Basketball?

What percent of 2nd lunch students prefer Basketball?

Based on the basketball percentages (40% vs 50%), is there an association?

What is the relative frequency of students who prefer Soccer (out of 120)?
30 out of 120 → 30/120 = 0.25 = 25% ✓

36 students prefer Other out of 120 total → 36/120 = 0.30 = 30% ✓

Question 1
The line y = −3.5x + 75 models temperature (°F) at altitude x (in 1,000s of feet). At 8,000 ft (x = 8), predict the temperature.
y = −3.5(8) + 75 = −28 + 75 = 47°F ✓. Each 1,000 ft of altitude drops temperature by 3.5°F.
Question 2
Using the same model (y = −3.5x + 75): at what altitude does the model predict 0°F? Round to the nearest 1,000 ft.
0 = −3.5x + 75 → 3.5x = 75 → x = 75/3.5 ≈ 21.4 → about 21,000 ft ✓
Question 3
Two researchers study "firefighters at scene vs. fire damage." They find a strong positive correlation. Researcher A says: "More firefighters cause MORE damage." Researcher B says: "More firefighters are sent to BIGGER fires, which also cause more damage." Who is correct?
This is the classic confounding variable problem. Fire SIZE is the hidden cause of both more firefighters AND more damage. Correlation ≠ causation! ✓
Question 4
A two-way table shows: 80% of athletes who do strength training pass fitness tests, vs. 40% of athletes who don't. The difference is 40 percentage points. What does this suggest?
A 40 percentage point difference is large — it strongly suggests an association between strength training and fitness test results. ✓
Question 5
Line of best fit: y = 12x + 5. If x increases by 3 units, by how much does y increase?
Each unit increase in x increases y by 12 (the slope). 3 unit increase → y increases by 12 × 3 = 36 ✓
Question 6
Can we use a scatterplot to PROVE that studying more CAUSES higher test scores? Explain.
Correlation never proves causation. High-scoring students may also attend tutoring, have more sleep, or have stronger background knowledge. A controlled experiment is needed to establish causation. ✓

🔍 Common Mistakes

Can you spot what went wrong?

Correlation: points go up-left to down-right
✗ Student: Student says: positive correlation
✓ Fix: WRONG! Downhill = NEGATIVE correlation. Uphill = positive.
💡 Think of it like a hill: going up = positive, going down = negative.

⚡ Key Tips for This Unit

✅ Positive: both increase. Negative: one up, other down. None: random.
✅ Line of best fit: approx equal points above and below.
✅ Outlier: far from the pattern. Can distort the trend line.
✅ Joint RF = cell÷grand total. Conditional RF = cell÷row or column total.
