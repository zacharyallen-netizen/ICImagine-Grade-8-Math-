Expressions & EquationsTopic 1NC.8.EE.1

Operating with monomials & exponents

A monomial is a single term: a number, a variable, or a product of them, like 7, x, or −4x³y. Before scientific notation and equations, lock down the exponent rules — they show up everywhere on the EOG.

The exponent rules

  • Product: same base → add exponents. xᵃ · xᵇ = xᵃ⁺ᵇ
  • Quotient: same base → subtract exponents. xᵃ ÷ xᵇ = xᵃ⁻ᵇ
  • Power of a power: multiply exponents. (xᵃ)ᵇ = xᵃᵇ
  • Zero: anything (≠0) to the 0 power is 1. x⁰ = 1
  • Negative: flip it. x⁻ᵃ = 1 / xᵃ
Worked example · multiply
Multiply (3x²)(4x⁵).
Coefficients: 3 · 4 = 12. Bases match, so add exponents: x²⁺⁵ = x⁷.
Answer: 12x⁷
Worked example · divide
Simplify (15x⁶y³) ÷ (5x²y).
Coefficients: 15 ÷ 5 = 3. Subtract exponents per base: x⁶⁻² = x⁴, y³⁻¹ = y².
Answer: 3x⁴y²
Watch out: coefficients are multiplied or divided; exponents are added or subtracted. Don't mix those operations up — that's the most common mistake on this standard.

x³ · x⁴ = x¹²

Simplify (2a³)(5a²).

Simplify (15x⁶y³) ÷ (5x²y). (Type using ^ for exponents, e.g. 3x^4y^2)

Scientific notation

Scientific notation writes very large or very small numbers compactly as a × 10ⁿ, where the coefficient a is at least 1 but less than 10, and n is an integer.

Operating in scientific notation

  • Compare: bigger exponent wins. If exponents tie, compare the coefficients.
  • Multiply: multiply the coefficients, add the exponents, then fix the coefficient to be 1–10.
  • Divide: divide the coefficients, subtract the exponents, then fix the coefficient.

Expressions & EquationsTopic 3NC.8.EE.7b

Multi-step linear equations

The hardest equations combine four things at once: the distributive property, like terms, variables on both sides, and fractions. The order of attack stays the same every time.

The order of operations for solving

  • 1. Clear fractions — multiply every term by the LCD.
  • 2. Distribute to remove parentheses.
  • 3. Combine like terms on each side.
  • 4. Get variables on one side, numbers on the other.
  • 5. Divide by the coefficient.
Worked example · distribute & both sides
3(2x − 4) = 2x + 8
Distribute: 6x − 12 = 2x + 8
Subtract 2x, add 12: 4x = 20
Divide by 4: x = 5
Worked example · clearing fractions
½x + 3 = ¼x + 5
Multiply every term by the LCD (4): 2x + 12 = x + 20
Subtract x, subtract 12: x = 8
Tip: when you distribute, the factor hits every term inside the parentheses — including the second one and its sign.

Solve 3(2x − 4) = 2x + 8.

Expressions & EquationsTopic 4NC.8.EE.7a

One, none, or infinite solutions & inequalities

When you solve an equation and the variables cancel out, the variable is no longer there to tell you the answer — what's left tells you how many solutions there are.

How many solutions?

  • One solution: you end with x = a number.
  • No solution: variables cancel and you're left with a false statement like 5 = 7.
  • Infinitely many: variables cancel and you're left with a true statement like 3 = 3 (both sides are identical).

Inequalities & their graphs

  • Solve exactly like an equation — but flip the inequality sign whenever you multiply or divide by a negative.
  • Open circle ○ for < or > (endpoint NOT included).
  • Closed circle ● for ≤ or ≥ (endpoint included).
  • Arrow points the way the solution goes: toward larger numbers for >, smaller for <.

How many solutions does 2(x + 3) = 2x + 6 have?

How many solutions does 4x + 1 = 4x − 3 have?

Ends as 2 = 9

GeometryTopic 5NC.8.G.5

Angles: parallel lines, transversals & triangles

When a transversal cuts two parallel lines, it creates eight angles with predictable relationships. Knowing which pairs are equal and which are supplementary lets you set up an equation and solve for a variable.

Angle pair relationships (when lines are parallel)

  • Corresponding (same position, e.g. ∠1 & ∠5): equal
  • Alternate interior (∠3 & ∠6): equal
  • Alternate exterior (∠1 & ∠8): equal
  • Same-side interior (∠3 & ∠5): supplementary (add to 180°)
  • Vertical (∠1 & ∠4): equal; linear pair (∠1 & ∠2): supplementary

Triangle angle theorems

  • Interior sum: the three interior angles add to 180°.
  • Exterior angle: an exterior angle equals the sum of the two remote (far-away) interior angles.

GeometryTopic 6NC.8.G.7 · NC.8.G.8

The Pythagorean Theorem

In any right triangle, a² + b² = c², where a and b are the legs and c is the hypotenuse — the longest side, always opposite the right angle.

Using the theorem

  • Find the hypotenuse: add the squared legs, then square-root. c = √(a² + b²)
  • Find a leg: SUBTRACT — a = √(c² − b²). (The hypotenuse must be by itself first.)
  • Distance between two points: the change in x and change in y are the two legs.
  • 3-D / space diagonal of a box: d = √(ℓ² + w² + h²).
Strategy for word problems: always sketch and label the situation first. Ladders, shadows, distances, and TV diagonals are all hidden right triangles — draw the triangle, mark which sides you know, and decide whether you're finding the hypotenuse or a leg.

A right triangle has legs 6 and 8. Find the hypotenuse.

The hypotenuse is 13 and one leg is 5. Find the other leg.

Error analysis. To find a leg when the hypotenuse is 10 and a leg is 6, a student computes √(10² + 6²). What's the mistake?

Sketch it: the ladder is the hypotenuse; the ground distance is a leg; the height is the other leg.

FunctionsTopic 7NC.8.F.1

Functions vs. relations

A relation is any set of input-output pairs. A function is a special relation with one strict rule: every input has exactly one output. An input can never point to two different outputs.

Four ways to show a function

  • Table — check that no x-value repeats with a different y.
  • Graph — use the vertical line test: if any vertical line crosses the graph more than once, it's not a function.
  • Equation — e.g. y = 3x − 2.
  • Mapping diagram or words — describe the input-output rule.
Key distinction: the same output can come from different inputs (that's fine). It's a repeated input with different outputs that breaks the function rule.

Which set of points is NOT a function?

A function is allowed to have two different inputs that produce the same output.

FunctionsTopic 8NC.8.F.2 · NC.8.EE.6

Slope & graphing linear functions

Slope measures steepness — how fast y changes as x changes. It's the same value whether you read it from a graph, a table, two points, or an equation.

Finding slope

  • Formula: m = (y₂ − y₁) / (x₂ − x₁) — change in y over change in x ("rise over run").
  • From a table: divide the change in y by the change in x between rows.
  • Positive rises left→right; negative falls; zero is horizontal; undefined is vertical.

Find the slope of the line through (2, 3) and (6, 11).

A table shows x: 0, 1, 2, 3 and y: 5, 8, 11, 14. Find the slope.

Modeling with slope-intercept form

Most linear word problems fit y = mx + b: m is the rate (the amount of change per step) and b is the starting value (where you begin, when x = 0).

Worked example
A gym charges a $20 sign-up fee plus $15 per month.
Rate m = 15, start b = 20 → y = 15x + 20.
Cost after 6 months: 15(6) + 20 = 110.

FunctionsTopic 10NC.8.F.4

What slope & y-intercept mean in context

Writing the equation is only half the job. The EOG loves to ask what the numbers mean in the real situation — and to have you solve for a rate or starting value from given facts.

Translating the numbers

  • Slope (m) = the rate of change — the "per" amount, with units (dollars per hour, liters per minute, cm per day).
  • y-intercept (b) = the starting value — the amount when x = 0 (before anything happens).
  • A specific point (x, y) = "at this input, the output is this" — e.g. (3, 90) means "at 3 hours, the cost is $90."

StatisticsTopic 11NC.8.SP.1 · NC.8.SP.2 · NC.8.SP.3

Scatter plots & the line of best fit

A scatter plot graphs paired data to reveal a relationship between two variables. You describe what you see in three ways: the association, the form, and any unusual points.

Describing a scatter plot

  • Positive association: y tends to rise as x rises. Negative: y falls as x rises. None: no pattern.
  • Form: linear (straight) or nonlinear (curved). Strength: tight points = strong; spread out = weak.
  • Outlier: a point far from the overall pattern. Cluster: a group of points bunched together.
  • Line of best fit: a straight line modeling the trend, used to make predictions. Its slope and y-intercept have real-world meaning.

As students study more hours, their test scores tend to increase. What association is this?

What is the main purpose of a line of best fit?

A line of best fit is y = 5x + 60 (score vs. study hours). Predict the score for 4 hours.

In that best-fit line y = 5x + 60, what does the slope 5 mean in context?

A negative association means that as one variable increases, the other tends to decrease.

A two-way frequency table sorts data by two categories at once. Reading it well — and knowing the vocabulary — is the whole standard.

Joint frequency
A count in an inner cell — combining both categories (e.g. owns a pet and plays a sport = 18).
Marginal frequency
A total in the margins — a row total or column total (e.g. 25 pet owners, 27 athletes).
Relative frequency
A count divided by a total, written as a fraction, decimal, or percent (e.g. 25 ÷ 40 = 0.625 = 62.5%).

GeometryTopic 13NC.8.G.1–4

Transformations: congruence & similarity

Transformations move or resize figures. The key question on the EOG: did the figure stay the same size (congruent) or just keep the same shape (similar)?

TransformationWhat it doesResult
Translation (slide)Moves every point the same distanceCongruent
Reflection (flip)Mirrors across a lineCongruent
Rotation (turn)Turns around a pointCongruent
Dilation (resize)Scales by a factor from a centerSimilar

Handy coordinate rules

  • Translation: (x, y) → (x + a, y + b)
  • Reflection over x-axis: (x, y) → (x, −y); over y-axis: (x, y) → (−x, y)
  • Rotation 180° about origin: (x, y) → (−x, −y)
  • Dilation by k about origin: (x, y) → (kx, ky)
Identifying a sequence: if the two figures are the same size, the sequence uses only rigid motions (slides, flips, turns). If one is a scaled version of the other, a dilation is part of the sequence — the figures are similar, not congruent.
