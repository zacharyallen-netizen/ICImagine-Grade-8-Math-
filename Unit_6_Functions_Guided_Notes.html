6.4

Linear Functions in Real-World Context

NC.8.F.4 — Construct a function to model a linear relationship; interpret rate of change and initial value in context.

0 of 0 filled
What is this section about? Real-world problems often follow a linear pattern — a steady rate of change plus a starting value. We can write equations in y = mx + b form to model these situations and use them to make predictions.

Slope & Y-Intercept in Context

In real-world problems, the slope and y-intercept have specific meanings:

m (slope) = the of change — how much y changes per unit of x.

b (y-intercept) = the value — what y equals when x = .

💡 Quick check: The y-intercept is always the "before-anything-happens" value — the initial cost, starting amount, or fixed fee.

Keywords for Slope and Y-Intercept

Slope keywords (rate):

  • per hour, per mile, per day
  • each, every
  • increases by, decreases by
  • price/cost per item

Y-intercept keywords (start):

  • flat fee, base cost
  • starting amount, initial
  • up-front, deposit
  • "to begin with"

Plumber example: A plumber charges $50 to come to your house, plus $30 per hour of work.

The flat fee ($50) is paid before any work happens → b = 50
The $30 per hour is the rate of change → m = 30
Equation: y = 30x + 50

Interpret: If x = 4 hours, then y = 30(4) + 50 = $170 total.

Cell phone plans: Plan A costs $20/month plus $0.05/minute. Plan B costs $30/month plus $0.03/minute.

Plan A: y = 0.05x + 20
Plan B: y = 0.03x + 30
At x = 0 minutes: Plan A = $20, Plan B = $30 → Plan A starts cheaper
At x = 500 minutes: Plan A = $45, Plan B = $45 → tied!
At x = 1000 minutes: Plan A = $70, Plan B = $60 → Plan B is cheaper for heavy use

Practice 2: Two savings accounts:

  • Account A starts with $300 and adds $25/week → y = 25x + 300
  • Account B starts with $400 and adds $20/week → y = 20x + 400

Which has more money after 10 weeks?

Account A after 10 weeks: y = 25() + 300 = $

Account B after 10 weeks: y = 20(10) + 400 = $

Account has more after 10 weeks.

🔑 Strategy: When word problems compare two situations, write both equations first, then plug in the same value of x to compare. Or, if asked about long-term, look at the slopes — steeper slope wins eventually.
6.6

Qualitative Graphs

NC.8.F.5 — Describe qualitatively the functional relationship between two quantities by analyzing a graph; sketch a graph from a verbal description.

0 of 0 filled
What's a qualitative graph? A graph that shows the shape of a relationship over time without exact numbers. The story of the graph matters more than specific values. Common examples: distance-time graphs, temperature-time graphs.

Reading the Shape

Each section of a qualitative graph tells you something. Going left-to-right, ask:

Going UP means the quantity is .

Going DOWN means the quantity is .

FLAT (horizontal) means the quantity is (not changing).

STEEPER lines mean the change is .

SHALLOWER (gentler) lines mean the change is .

Maria rides her bike to school:

The graph shows Maria's distance from home over time. We can read the story:

  • 0 to 4 min: line goes UP at a steady rate → Maria is biking away from home toward school.
  • 4 to 6 min: line is FLAT → Maria stays at school (distance doesn't change).
  • 6 to 9 min: line goes DOWN steeper than it went up → Maria rides home faster than she went.
⚠️ Common mistake: When someone is returning home, the distance-from-home line goes DOWN (toward zero), not back up! The y-axis is "distance from start" — if you're getting closer to home, your distance is decreasing.

Practice 1: A swimmer dives into a pool, swims at a steady pace to the far wall, rests for a moment, then swims back faster than she went.

The graph below should show distance from start versus time. Match each phase to the correct description:

Phase 1 (going up at steady rate): she swam to the far wall at a steady pace.

Phase 2 (flat line): she at the far wall.

Phase 3 (going down steeper): she swam faster than she went.

A pot of water: A pot of water is placed on a stove. It heats slowly at first, then more rapidly, stays at boiling for several minutes, then is removed and cools gradually.

  • Phase 1 (shallow up): heats slowly
  • Phase 2 (steep up): heats more rapidly
  • Phase 3 (flat): at boiling, temperature stays constant
  • Phase 4 (down): removed from stove, cools off gradually

Practice 2: Look at a distance-time graph with three phases:

  • Phase 1 (0–3 min): steep upward line
  • Phase 2 (3–6 min): flat line
  • Phase 3 (6–10 min): gradual upward line

Phase 1: the person is moving (away from start).

Phase 2: the person is (not moving).

Phase 3: the person is moving (still away from start).

📐 Steps for sketching from a story:
  1. Break the story into 2–4 phases.
  2. For each phase, decide: increase, decrease, or stay the same?
  3. Decide if the change is fast (steep) or slow (shallow).
  4. Connect phases at their endpoints — no jumps in the line!
