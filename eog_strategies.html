🎯 EOG Test Strategies

Math · English · Science — Strategies, formulas, vocabulary, and practice for the NC End-of-Grade test.

🌙 Before the Test

😴 Sleep is your secret weapon

8+ hours — no exceptions. Cramming the night before costs more than it gains. Your brain consolidates memories during sleep, which means pulling an all-nighter actually removes access to things you already know. If you've been studying, trust it. Sleep.

🍳 Eat a real breakfast

Protein + complex carbs: eggs, toast, oatmeal, fruit, yogurt. These digest slowly and keep blood sugar steady. Avoid pure-sugar options (donuts, sugary cereal) — you'll get an energy spike followed by a crash right in the middle of the test.

💧 Hydrate intentionally

Drink water the morning of — even mild dehydration causes brain fog and makes it harder to focus. Skip the energy drinks; the caffeine crash is real and will hit during the test. One moderate cup of coffee is fine if you're used to it; don't introduce something new on test day.

✏️ Know your supplies in advance

  • 2+ sharpened #2 pencils and a good eraser
  • Water bottle (check if allowed at your desk)
  • Ask your teacher NOW: calculator allowed? Reference sheet provided? Scratch paper?
  • Know where the test is and what time to be there — no last-minute scrambles

📚 Do a "light review" — not a cram session

The night before, flip through your notes or formula sheet for 20–30 minutes max. You're not learning new things — you're warming up pathways you already built. Then stop and rest. The brain needs downtime to lock things in.

🧘 Manage test anxiety before you walk in

  • Box breathing: Inhale 4 counts, hold 4, exhale 4, hold 4. Repeat 3x. It literally slows your heart rate.
  • Reframe nerves as readiness: Anxiety and excitement feel the same physically. Tell yourself "I'm fired up" instead of "I'm scared."
  • Arrive a few minutes early so you're not rushing — rushed brains make rushed errors.
🧠 Mindset shift: Walk in saying "I've prepared, I know this material." Research shows positive self-talk measurably improves test performance. Fake confidence until you feel it — your brain will follow.

⚡ During the Test

📖 Read the question TWICE — then look at answers

Every wrong answer is engineered to look right if you read too fast. The test writers know the most common mistakes, and they put those mistakes in the answer choices. Slow down on the question stem, then attack the answers.

🔑 Find the key words in the question

Before choosing an answer, underline or mentally flag the most important words in the question. Words like BEST, MOST likely, primary purpose, NOT, and EXCEPT completely change what you're looking for. Miss a key word, miss the question.

❌ Eliminate wrong answers before picking right ones

  • Kill answers with extreme words: always, never, all, none, only, must
  • Kill answers that are off-topic (don't match the question)
  • Kill answers that contradict facts you know or the passage
  • Now choose from what's left — you've narrowed the odds dramatically

🔍 "Which is BEST?" — there are often two good answers

On questions asking for the BEST answer, expect to find two answers that are partially correct. The right answer is the one that's more complete, more specific, or more directly supported. Ask yourself: "Does this answer the exact question, or does it just sound related?"

🏁 Mark and move on

Stuck for 90 seconds? Put your best guess, circle the question number, and keep going. Come back at the end. One hard question isn't worth losing time on 3 easy ones you haven't reached yet. You need every point.

🔄 Trust your first instinct — but check your work

Research shows your first answer is correct more often than a changed answer. ONLY change if you catch a real error: a math calculation mistake, a misread question, or new information from a later question that clarifies this one. Never change because you "feel unsure." That's just nerves.

✏️ Use scratch paper aggressively

  • Math: Write every step. Don't do mental math on multi-step problems.
  • Science: Draw diagrams of setups, label forces, sketch particle models.
  • ELA: Jot a 3–5 word summary of each paragraph as you read.
  • Offload your thinking to the page — your brain has limited working memory. Don't make it store and calculate at the same time.

🧠 If your mind goes blank, don't panic

  • Take 3 slow breaths — this genuinely helps (lowers cortisol)
  • Skip the question and move on; come back with fresh eyes
  • Start writing down anything you know about the topic — often the answer unlocks once you get the brain moving
  • Use process of elimination even if you have no idea — you can still narrow it to 50/50
💡 Never leave blanks. A blank is a guaranteed zero. If you can eliminate even one wrong answer, guess from what remains. On a 4-choice question you have a 25–50% chance of a free point.

🪤 Common Traps

⚠️ EXCEPT / NOT questions. Read twice. You're picking the answer that is WRONG or DIFFERENT — not the true one.

🎣 The "almost right" answer

Test writers put an answer that's 90% correct — wrong unit, flipped sign, one word too extreme. Read every word of every answer.

🔢 Wrong units in math

Asked for area but you calculated perimeter? Your number is still in the choices. Always ask: did I answer what was asked? Are units right (cm vs cm²)?

📚 "Best supported by the passage"

Some answers are true in real life but NOT in the passage. Find words in the text that prove your answer. If you can't point to them, it's wrong.

⚠️ If an answer seems way too obvious, re-read the question. Sometimes it really IS obvious — but sometimes there's a twist.

⏱️ Time Management

📊 Calculate your pace before you start

  • 50 questions in 75 min = 1.5 min per question
  • Check the clock every 10 questions
  • Behind? Speed up on easy ones; flag the hard ones to skip

🟢 Easy → Medium → Hard

First pass: obvious answers. Second pass: questions that need thought. Third pass: ones you circled.

🎯 Save 5 minutes to check

  • Every question has an answer (even if you guessed)
  • Name is on the test
  • Any math you flagged to verify
✅ Never leave blanks. Wrong guess = 0 pts. Guess = up to 25% chance of a point.

🎯 Math Strategies by Topic

Real Numbers & Square Roots (U1)

Estimating √ of non-perfect squares

  • Find the two perfect squares sandwiching your number
  • Ex: √50 → 49<50<64 → between 7 and 8, closer to 7
  • Algorithm: lower root + (n−lower²)÷(upper²−lower²)
Trap: √ that comes out clean (√25=5) is RATIONAL. Only √ of non-perfect-squares is irrational.

Exponents & Scientific Notation (U2)

Exponent rules at a glance

  • Multiplying same base → ADD exponents
  • Dividing same base → SUBTRACT exponents
  • Power to power → MULTIPLY exponents
  • Negative exponent → flip to fraction (x⁻² = 1/x²)
  • Zero exponent → anything⁰ = 1
Scientific notation: coefficient must be ≥1 and <10. After multiplying, adjust if needed.

Equations (U3)

One, none, or infinite solutions?

  • x = number → one solution
  • Variables cancel, nonsense remains (3=7) → no solution
  • Variables cancel, truth remains (5=5) → infinite solutions

Angles & Triangles (U4)

Parallel lines cut by a transversal

  • Corresponding / Alternate interior / Alternate exterior → EQUAL
  • Same-side interior (co-interior) → ADD to 180°
  • Triangle interior angles → always 180°
  • Exterior angle = sum of the two remote interior angles

Pythagorean Theorem (U5)

a² + b² = c²

  • c is always the hypotenuse (across from right angle, longest side)
  • Finding hypotenuse: add the squares, take root
  • Finding a leg: subtract the squares, take root
  • Draw the triangle first in word problems!
#1 error: If c is given, you're solving for a leg — subtract, don't add.

Functions & Systems (U6)

Key checks

  • Slope from 2 points: (y₂−y₁)÷(x₂−x₁)
  • Function test: each x has exactly ONE y
  • Systems: 1 intersection = 1 solution; parallel = none; same line = infinite

Scatter Plots (U7)

Describing association

  • Positive: as x↑, y↑ | Negative: as x↑, y↓ | None: random scatter
  • Linear vs. nonlinear: can you draw a straight line through the middle?

Transformations (U8)

Coordinate rules

  • Reflect over x-axis: (x,y)→(x,−y)
  • Reflect over y-axis: (x,y)→(−x,y)
  • Rotate 180°: (x,y)→(−x,−y)
  • Dilate by k: (x,y)→(kx,ky)
  • Translate: add/subtract from x and y

Volume (U9)

Formulas

  • Cylinder: V = πr²h
  • Cone: V = (1/3)πr²h
  • Sphere: V = (4/3)πr³
#1 volume error: Using diameter instead of radius. If given diameter, divide by 2 first.

📚 ELA Reading Strategies

👀 Preview questions first

Skim the question stems (not answers) before reading. Your brain will catch key details on the first pass instead of searching three times.

✏️ Annotate while reading

  • Circle unfamiliar words (use context clues later)
  • Underline the main idea / thesis
  • ★ Star important details, character actions, tone shifts
  • 3-5 word margin notes beside each paragraph

Question Types

Central Idea vs. Theme

Central idea = what the text is ABOUT (a topic). Theme = the LESSON or MESSAGE (a complete statement).

Inference

Answer is implied, not stated. Look for clues → ask "what can I REASONABLY conclude from these words?" Wrong answers make leaps the text doesn't support.

Vocabulary in Context — 5 clue types

  • Definition: "lethargic — tired and sluggish"
  • Synonym: nearby word with similar meaning
  • Antonym: contrast clue ("unlike her jubilant sister, she was morose")
  • Example: "Carnivores, like lions, eat meat"
  • Inference: use tone and situation

Text Structure

  • Chronological: first, then, finally
  • Cause/Effect: because, so, as a result
  • Compare/Contrast: similarly, however, unlike
  • Problem/Solution: problem, solution, resolve

"Best supported by the passage"

Find the specific line(s). If you can't point to words in the text, it's probably wrong. Outside knowledge doesn't count.

⚠️ Outside knowledge trap: The answer is what THIS text says — not what you know about the topic from class.

📋 Literary Terms Sheet

Figurative Language — Comparisons

Simile: comparison using "like" or "as"
Metaphor: direct comparison without "like/as"
Personification: human traits on non-human things
Hyperbole: extreme exaggeration
Analogy: extended comparison

Sound & Word Choice

Alliteration: repeated consonant sounds at word start
Onomatopoeia: word sounds like what it means (buzz)
Imagery: language appealing to the 5 senses
Idiom: non-literal expression ("break a leg")

Meaning & Layers

Symbolism: object representing a bigger idea
Irony: opposite of what's expected
Allusion: reference to another work or event
Foreshadowing: hint about what comes later
Flashback: scene jumping back in time

Tone & Mood

Tone: author's attitude (sarcastic, hopeful, angry)
Mood: feeling the reader gets (suspenseful, eerie)
Connotation: what a word implies beyond its definition
Denotation: dictionary definition

Story Structure

Exposition: intro — characters, setting, background
Rising action: events building tension
Climax: peak / turning point
Falling action: after the climax
Resolution: how it ends

Point of View

1st person: narrator is a character — uses "I"
3rd person limited: narrator knows ONE character's thoughts
3rd person omniscient: narrator knows ALL thoughts

Conflict Types

vs. Self: internal struggle
vs. Character: conflict between people
vs. Nature: storm, animals, environment
vs. Society: cultural norms or systems

🧪 Science Strategies

📊 Always check axes AND units on graphs

X and Y labels + units (seconds vs minutes, grams vs kg) change everything. Read both axes before answering.

🔎 Identify variables

  • Independent: what scientist CHANGES (x-axis)
  • Dependent: what they MEASURE (y-axis)
  • Control: what stays the same

🎯 Stick to the data — don't extrapolate

If a chart shows results at 20°, 30°, and 40°, you cannot conclude what happens at 50°. Conclusions must stay within what was measured.

Common Question Types

🧬 Punnett squares

  • Big letters = dominant; small = recessive
  • Homozygous = same (BB/bb); heterozygous = different (Bb)
  • Count out of 4 squares for percentages
  • Phenotype = what you see; genotype = the allele code

⚡ Newton's Laws

  • 1st: objects stay at rest/motion unless a force acts
  • 2nd: F = ma
  • 3rd: equal and opposite reactions
  • Balanced forces = no acceleration

🌊 Waves

  • Mechanical (sound): needs a medium
  • Electromagnetic (light): no medium needed; travels through space
  • Higher frequency = shorter wavelength (inverse relationship)
  • Amplitude = energy

🌍 Earth & Rocks

  • Superposition: older layers on BOTTOM
  • Convergent: plates push together → mountains, trenches
  • Divergent: plates pull apart → rifts, new crust
  • Transform: slide past → earthquakes

🦋 Ecosystems

  • Mutualism: both benefit (+/+)
  • Commensalism: one benefits, other unaffected (+/0)
  • Parasitism: one benefits, other harmed (+/−)
  • Only ~10% of energy passes to next level

⚗️ Matter & Chemistry (PS.8.1)

  • Atom structure: protons (+, in nucleus), neutrons (neutral, in nucleus), electrons (−, orbiting). Atomic number = # of protons.
  • Periodic table: elements arranged by atomic number; rows = periods; columns = groups (similar properties). The test provides the periodic table.
  • Element vs. compound vs. mixture: Element = one type of atom (O₂). Compound = atoms chemically bonded (H₂O). Mixture = physically combined, can be separated (salt water).
  • Physical change: form changes, substance stays the same (ice melting, paper tearing)
  • Chemical change: new substance forms — clues: color change, gas produced, energy released/absorbed, precipitate forms (burning, rusting, cooking)

⚖️ Law of Conservation of Mass

  • In a chemical reaction, atoms are rearranged, not created or destroyed
  • Mass of reactants = mass of products (always)
  • Balanced equations: same number of each type of atom on both sides
  • If a question gives you reactant mass, the products must equal the same total mass

🦎 Biological Evolution (LS.8.3)

  • Natural selection: individuals with traits better suited to environment survive and reproduce more → those traits become more common over generations
  • Variation: individuals within a species differ; variation is the raw material for evolution
  • Adaptation: a heritable trait that increases survival/reproduction in an environment
  • Evidence of evolution: fossil record, comparative anatomy (homologous structures), DNA similarities
  • Comparative anatomy: similar bone structures in different species (human arm, whale flipper, bat wing) → suggest common ancestor
  • Vestigial structures: reduced/unused structures left over from ancestors (human tailbone, whale hip bones)

🌙 Earth's Place in the Universe

  • Moon phases: caused by the moon's position relative to Earth and Sun — we see different amounts of the lit side
  • Seasons: caused by Earth's tilt, not its distance from the Sun — summer = your hemisphere tilted toward the Sun
  • Tides: caused by the Moon's gravitational pull on Earth's oceans
  • Solar vs. lunar eclipse: solar = Moon between Earth and Sun (daytime). Lunar = Earth between Sun and Moon (nighttime, full moon).

🌱 Earth and Human Activity

  • Human impacts: deforestation, fossil fuel burning, agriculture, and urbanization affect climate, water quality, and biodiversity
  • Greenhouse effect: CO₂ and other gases trap heat in the atmosphere — increasing concentrations = warming trend
  • Renewable vs. nonrenewable: solar/wind/hydro = renewable; coal/oil/natural gas = nonrenewable (finite, produce CO₂)
  • Resource management: conservation, recycling, and sustainable practices reduce human impact
⚠️ Mass ≠ Weight. Mass is constant (kg). Weight = force of gravity on mass (Newtons). On the moon, less weight, same mass.
⚠️ Physical vs. Chemical change trap: Dissolving (salt in water) = physical change — the salt is still there, just dispersed. Burning = chemical change — new substances form.
