Scatterplots · Correlation · Line of Best Fit · Two-Way Tables · NC.8.SP.1–4

7.1 Scatterplots & Correlation
📌 Scatterplot: A graph that shows the relationship between two numerical variables. Each point (x, y) represents one piece of data for two measurements of the same subject.
📌 Bivariate Data: Data involving exactly two variables measured from the same subjects — for example, hours studied and test score for each student in a class.

Types of Correlation

⚠️ Correlation ≠ Causation! Just because two variables are related does NOT mean one causes the other. Ice cream sales and drowning rates are positively correlated — but ice cream doesn't cause drowning. Both increase in summer (the real cause).

Describing a Scatterplot

When describing a scatterplot, address all four of these features:

📝 The Four Features

1. Direction — positive, negative, or none?
2. Strength — strong, moderate, or weak?
3. Form — linear (straight) or nonlinear (curved)?
4. Outliers — any points far from the pattern?

✏️ Sample Description

"The scatterplot shows a strong,
positive, linear association.
There is one outlier at (7, 30)
that does not fit the trend."

✓ Check: A scatterplot shows that as temperature rises, hot chocolate sales fall. What type of correlation is this?

7.2 Line of Best Fit
📌 Line of Best Fit (Trend Line): A straight line drawn through a scatterplot that best represents the linear trend. It should have roughly equal numbers of data points above and below it.

Drawing the Line

Using the Equation of the Line (NC.8.SP.3)

📌 Equation of Line of Best Fit: Written as y = mx + b, just like any linear equation.
• m (slope) = the rate of change — how much y changes per 1-unit increase in x
• b (y-intercept) = the starting value — what y equals when x = 0

Example: Interpreting the Equation y = 8x + 52

Context: x = hours studied, y = test score
Slope = 8 → for every 1 extra hour studied, the score increases by about 8 points
Intercept = 52 → a student who studies 0 hours is predicted to score about 52 points
Predict score for 5 hours: y = 8(5) + 52 = 40 + 52 = 92
A student who studies 5 hours is predicted to score about 92 points ✓

Example: Interpolation vs. Extrapolation

Interpolation: predicting WITHIN the data range → more reliable
Extrapolation: predicting OUTSIDE the data range → less reliable
If data covers hours 1–6, predicting for 4 hours = interpolation ✓
Predicting for 15 hours = extrapolation — use with caution! ⚠️

✓ Check: The line of best fit for a dataset is y = 3x + 10. What does the slope of 3 mean in context (x = hours of exercise per week, y = pounds lost)?

7.3 Two-Way Frequency Tables (NC.8.SP.4)
📌 Two-Way Frequency Table: A table that organizes data for two categorical variables at once. Rows represent one category, columns represent another. The margins (edges) show totals.

Vocabulary

Joint Frequency: The values in the interior cells (not totals). They represent the count for a specific combination of both categories. Example: 18 students who attended tutoring AND passed.
Marginal Frequency: The row and column totals in the margins. They show the total for one category. Example: 40 total students, 30 students who passed.
Relative Frequency: A joint or marginal frequency expressed as a fraction or percentage of a total. Formula: relative frequency = part ÷ total

Reading a Two-Way Table

Passed QuizFailed QuizRow Total
Attended Tutoring18220
Did Not Attend12820
Column Total301040

Example Questions from This Table

1. What is the joint freq. of tutoring students who passed? → 18 (interior cell)
2. What is the marginal freq. of all students who passed? → 30 (column total)
3. What % of tutoring students passed? → 18 ÷ 20 = 0.90 = 90%
4. What % of all students failed? → 10 ÷ 40 = 0.25 = 25%
5. Is there an association? YES — tutoring students passed at 90% vs 60% without. That's a meaningful difference, suggesting an association!

Identifying Association

📌 Association in Categorical Data: If the relative frequencies within rows (or columns) differ noticeably between groups, there may be an association between the two variables. If the percentages are nearly the same, the variables appear independent (no association).

Example: Phone Preference by Grade

7th grade: 35/50 = 70% prefer iPhone
8th grade: 20/50 = 40% prefer iPhone
There IS an association — grade level and phone preference appear related (70% vs 40% is a big difference).

🔍 Spot the Error: Correlation vs. Causation

✗ Student says: "Ice cream sales and drowning rates have a positive correlation, so eating ice cream causes drowning."
✓ Fix: Correlation does NOT mean causation! Both increase in summer because of a lurking variable — hot weather. Ice cream doesn't cause drowning!
💡 Always look for a third variable that could explain the relationship. Two things trending together doesn't mean one causes the other.

🔍 Spot the Error: Line of Best Fit

✗ Student says: "The line of best fit must pass through every data point."
✓ Fix: The line of best fit shows the general trend. It should have roughly equal numbers of points above and below it — it does NOT need to pass through any specific points.

🔍 Spot the Error: Reading a Two-Way Table

✗ Student says: "More 8th graders like basketball (35) than 7th graders (30), so 8th graders are more into basketball." But 8th grade has 80 students total vs 70 in 7th grade!
✓ Fix: Use relative frequency to compare fairly: 7th = 30/70 ≈ 42.9% vs 8th = 35/80 ≈ 43.75%. They're nearly the same! Raw counts can be misleading when group sizes differ.
💡 When comparing groups of different sizes, ALWAYS use percentages (relative frequency), not raw counts.
